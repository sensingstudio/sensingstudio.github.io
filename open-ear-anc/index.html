Virtual In-Ear Sensing

ANC for open-ear glasses with no in-ear mic: an array of frame microphones estimates the sound at your ear from the fixed head geometry, and the speaker plays anti-noise to cancel it. This simulates one side — the left ear (the real system runs a mirror-image copy for the right); mic count is per side. Set parameters on the left; watch the scene, spectrum, and live stats on the right. Headphones recommended.

What you're looking at

  • Normal ANC puts an error mic in the ear canal to measure leftover noise. Open-ear devices leave the ear uncovered, so that mic is impossible — the system has to infer the ear signal instead.
  • Because the glasses hold a fixed geometry relative to your head, the sound at the ear is a predictable mix of what the frame mics hear. A set of filters (one per mic) learned from that geometry reconstructs the ear signal — virtual in-ear sensing. The anti-noise is just the negative of that estimate.
  • The filters are trained across a population of head shapes, so they generalize to a new user without retraining — but the population average caps the achievable reduction (~10 dB). A brief calibration of your ear’s speaker→ear response recovers the rest (to ~11 dB).
  • Drag the source anywhere and the reduction barely changes — the array covers all directions. That spatial robustness is what lets it work under mobility, unlike fixed quiet-zone methods.
  • It cancels low frequencies well and high frequencies poorly: short wavelengths vary too fast across the head to predict from the frame. Conveniently, that’s also where most environmental noise isn’t — buses, engines, and HVAC live in the 100–1000 Hz band.
  • From the paper 8 MEMS mics + 2 open-ear speakers on a 3D-printed frame; a neural net updates the filters every 200 ms while a DSP applies them at 113 µs end-to-end latency. Measured: 9.6 dB mean reduction, 11.2 dB with calibration.
